## The three online platforms every venue must be on

You need an active presence on **Facebook**, **Instagram**, and **Google** (your Google Business Profile). These are the three places people most commonly look for a venue.

### Why Facebook
- **Credibility and connection.** People routinely search businesses on Facebook before visiting. If they can't find you, trust drops.
- **An information source.** Customers look there for current trading hours, menus and promotions — keep them accurate.
- **Events.** Don't be disheartened by low "Going" counts. Facebook events work two ways: as a reminder to people who've seen it, and as discovery — when locals browse "events near me", your event shows for your geographic area. Visibility is the goal, not RSVP numbers.
- **Still free to promote and sell.** You can run Valentine's specials, flash sales, etc. organically without paying for ads. You'd be crazy not to use the free space.

### Why Instagram (and the TikTok caveat)
At the time of this session, TikTok faced a potential US ban (decision due 19 Jan 2025). The prediction: if US celebrities/influencers leave, Australian audiences drift back to Instagram — so have Instagram "locked and loaded" first.

Instagram priorities:
- **Real-time updates and insights.** Treat Instagram like a daily blog. Staff farewells, a burst pipe in the kitchen, anything genuinely happening — share it. Real-time content gets higher views, and the *next* thing you post (a promotion, online ordering push) rides that lift. Tactic: whenever something real happens and views spike, schedule a sales/promo post immediately after.
- **Optimise the link in bio.** One link (or a Link tree) where the *first* option is your most profitable action — dine-in bookings or direct online ordering. End posts with a call to action pointing to it ("Click the link in our bio to book a table").
- **Treat Instagram as a search engine.** Your handle is your business name; your *account name* (the bold name field) should be your keywords — location + offering. Examples: a North Brisbane cafe uses "North Brisbane Cafe" so it surfaces when people search "Brisbane cafe"; a Sydney kebab shop should use "Tastiest Kebabs in Sydney". (Full walkthrough in file 04.)
- **Collaborate.** Invite food bloggers/influencers in. Digital word of mouth converts better than your own promo posts — people trust other people's recommendations.

### How to find food bloggers and influencers
- Search hashtags: your capital city + "eats" (e.g. #canberraeats), or "[your suburb] cafes", "[suburb] foodies", or look at who has posted "top 5 cafes in [suburb]" content and who follows them.
- DM them: "G'day, we're known for X, Y, Z and we'd love to have you in to experience it." Offer **contra** — food and drink in exchange for content.
- **Always use a one-page agreement** stating exactly what you get in return (stories, reels, posts) and that you receive the raw footage to reuse later. Without clear, signed deliverables you can spend $40 of food cost for one disappearing story. (Influencer workflow detail in file 06.)

### Why Google
- Google is the largest search engine; for high-intent purchases like catering and functions, **Google Ads typically beat Facebook ads** — people searching Google are ready to book *now*; by the time Facebook retargets them they've often already booked elsewhere, wasting your ad spend.
- Even without ads, an optimised Google Business Profile ranks you for keywords. Keep it current: set a monthly reminder (1st of the month) to update special holiday hours (e.g. Australia Day), respond to every review, and upload fresh photos (wide venue shots, staple menu items). More activity = Google sees you as valuable = higher ranking. (Full Google playbook in file 05.)

## Website health check (summary)

Over ~70% of Australian consumers (higher in the US, ~80%+) search for restaurants on mobile — yet most owners build and check their site on desktop. Audit on a phone: menu link working and findable, booking system properly integrated with **no orphaned old booking links**, online-ordering button visible at the top, pictures current, and the owners visible ("husband-and-wife owned" builds trust — it signals family business, not corporation). One owner's revenue rose **38% after adding a website** to a venue that had traded without one. Full website audit checklist in file 05.

## Delivery partners (summary)

Reframe: delivery platforms are **not there to make you money — they're there to tell the world you exist**. Treat them like a beautiful catalogue: great photos, accurate descriptions, prices increased ~30% as a "convenience tax", and your own direct online ordering promoted everywhere ("Order direct and save ~30%"). Stop funding free-delivery and BOGOF promos — the commission maths usually leaves you ~$0–1 per order. ## Gimmicks are back (and they make money)

**Definition:** a gimmick is a commercialised holiday or trend — Valentine's Day, National Donut Day, National Margarita Day, a viral drink — that gives you a reason to run a limited time offer.

Why gimmicks work:
- **Dopamine.** Limited-time, novel items excite people to buy.
- **Social status.** When a gimmick matches your target audience, they share it because it makes them look good to their peers — giant cocktails, a 2 kg parmigiana challenge people happily pay $50 for *because* they'll photograph it.
- **Proof at scale.** McDonald's Grimace (purple) shake sold out Australia-wide on day one. Sabrina Carpenter's brown sugar shaken espresso collaboration with Dunkin' spiked so hard that Starbucks stopped trending in the US. A member ran a book-launch event around *Onyx Storm* (riding the BookTok trend) and essentially sold out.

How to use them:
- Build promotions **4–6 weeks out** from the gimmick/event.
- Pick gimmicks that align with your venue and target audience (a bar leans into National Margarita Day; a grunge bar creates a drink for a touring rock band).
- Make the LTO profitable — gimmicks are an excuse for margin, not discounting.
- A holiday/gimmick trend calendar is produced for members each year — use it to plan.

## In-house marketing: monetise the customers already inside

- **TV displays.** Set a monthly reminder to refresh promo screens. You paid for the TVs and the power — make them sell. Show smiling photos from past functions and screenshots of customers' Instagram stories (social proof).
- **Table talkers.** People waiting at a table will read anything in front of them (the "reading the air-freshener can" effect). Use that attention: trivia nights, cake tastings, happy-hour upgrades, LTOs. Great for lifting ACS.
- **QR-code ordering.** Removes social anxiety — ordering from a phone is private, so people add the cheesy garlic bread, the prawn topper, the double shot they wouldn't say out loud. Privacy = higher ACS.
- **Loyalty cards.** They still work. Example programme: 5th drink half price, 10th drink free. A small suburban cafe went through 4,000 cards in a year. Smart twist: the coffee supplier funds the discounts — each half-price/free coffee is rung to a discount account, tallied monthly, and the supplier covers it because the card builds a daily purchasing habit. Ask: which supplier relationship could fund a loyalty card so it costs you nothing?
- **Flyers — on your own orders only.** Put next month's specials in/on bags for orders placed through *your* website to build hype ahead of time. **Never** put flyers in delivery-partner bags — platforms will cancel your account permanently if caught.
- **POS / till screens.** The customer-facing screen (Square and similar systems) is read constantly while people wait. Program upsells: "Add mushroom sauce $3", "Add a scoop of ice cream + cream to your iced latte $2.50", "Add a hash brown / prawn topper".
- **Posters.** Events can't live only on Facebook. Put them on walls, tables, near the till — wherever eyes already go.

## Giveaways and competitions: objectives or don't bother

No more "tag a friend" posts hoping something happens. Every giveaway must have one of two objectives:
1. **Grow your following** — because you're about to run a promotion or ad to that bigger audience; or
2. **Increase ACS** — e.g. "Spend $50 in February to enter the draw for X".

Rules:
- Comments for comments' sake cost money and time and achieve nothing.
- **Always publicly announce the winner** (photo of them collecting/enjoying the prize). Social proof that you follow through is why your *next* giveaway gets a bigger turnout.

## EGC — Employee Generated Content

You cannot create all the content yourself without burning out. Hand pieces of content creation to your team:
- **Incentivise it.** $2–$5 per published piece toward the staff Christmas party, or a pizza for whoever films the video — content will flood in.
- **Why it works.** Customers build connection and friendship with your staff; people buy from people they like, and they come in to see familiar faces.
- **Set standards.** One document covering brand guidelines (e.g. "no swearing — our audience includes mums and school kids"), read and signed by everyone, with clear repercussions for misuse written into contracts.
- **Access control.** Give managers trust-level access. Use the **Meta Business Suite app**: invite by email and edit permissions so staff can respond to comments, reshare stories and answer the inbox — but cannot deactivate the account or touch ad billing. One designated work phone logged into Instagram also works.

### Q: I run a premium, highly styled feed. Won't EGC dilute the aesthetic?
A: Keep the grid curated and let EGC live where it doesn't touch the grid: post staff reels **without showing them on the profile feed** (reels can be shared to the Reels tab only), and use **stories** (gone in 24 hours). You keep aesthetic control while still getting the reach and relatability — a friendly face or behind-the-scenes clip attracts more customers than another perfect cocktail photo.

## 2025 marketing predictions

1. **Hyper-personalisation.** Offers that speak directly to your audience's specific interests (the gimmick principle, sharpened). A grunge bar making a drink for a touring rock band.
2. **The rise of AI.** Use ChatGPT for automated/assisted review responses and audience research: "My target audience is mums aged 25–35 — list 10 things they're currently interested in." Or: "I want to create a 2025 marketing strategy — ask me 20 questions you need to know about my business first." The more detail you feed it (keep the chat open), the better the captions and ideas it returns.
3. **Sustainability.** Use moments like Earth Hour not to close for an hour but to *tell people what you already do*: solar panels installed, reduced trading hours cutting energy use, fabric napkins instead of disposable, donating packaging to women's shelters.
4. **Short-form content dominates.** It doesn't need to be elaborate — a slow-mo knife through an eggs-benedict croissant, ice dropping into a cup, a slow beer pour. Seconds-long, sensory clips get strong views and engagement.
5. **Collaborations.** Big brands are doing it (McDonald's × Krispy Kreme). Find a nearby business with a similar target market and cross-promote: if you each have 2,000 followers, ~1,000 of theirs likely don't know you exist. Ideas: co-hosted events; partner with a local gym/F45 running a challenge — "we'll build four challenge-friendly breakfasts for your members for six weeks" (fitness-challenge participants typically have higher disposable income).

## Kickoff Q&A

**Q: Hot tips on simple filming equipment — tripods, lights, microphones — to enhance an iPhone?**
A: Turn on the **PAL** setting in iPhone camera settings — it matches your frame rate to artificial lighting so video doesn't flicker. Light source comes from *behind you* (behind the camera), lighting the subject. Skip cheap microphones — used badly they create feedback and sound worse than the phone mic. For ASMR-style food videos, cheat: add free sound overlays in CapCut.

**Q: Is there an algorithm penalty for scheduling posts well in advance (e.g. a month ahead)?**
A: No — Meta's own Business Suite has a content planner, so scheduling itself isn't penalised. The penalty comes from scheduling and then **ghosting**. If nobody responds to comments, engages with stories, or interacts with other accounts, performance drops. Schedule freely, but be present: it's called *social* media.

**Q: Almost 40% of our sales come through Uber Eats and we run promotions on it. Should we stop?**
A: Check what the promotions cost you per month. If promo spend exceeds ~10% of your delivery revenue — on top of ~30% COGS, plus wages and rent — you're likely making $0–$1 per order. Remember Uber charges its full commission on the pre-discount order value and doesn't fund your free-delivery or BOGOF offers. Run the numbers before continuing. (Full breakdown in file 05.)

**Q: Can I remove poor-quality customer photos from my Google profile?**
A: You can't delete them directly, but you can **report** any customer photo (three-dot menu). Google reviews it and removes it if they agree it's unsuitable.

**Q: Best way to use ChatGPT for marketing?**
A: Treat it like an underpaid staff member. Have it interview *you* ("ask me 20 questions about my business"), keep the chat open so it retains context, then ask for outputs: "Based on what you know about my audience, write an engaging Valentine's caption that drives bookings for our heart-shaped brownie." Detail in = quality out.
