## Why content fails (the common frustrations and mistakes)

What owners say: "I post every day but nobody sees it." "My content looks good but doesn't translate to sales." "I don't know what to post or how often." (Looking good ≠ being wanted — Range Rovers are beautiful; most people still won't buy one.)

The underlying mistakes:
1. **Random content with no strategy** — no customer-nurturing journey, no relationship-building, no invitation to visit.
2. **Over-promotion** — buy, buy, buy to an audience you never engaged. Result: posting to crickets.
3. **Neglect** — venues with incredible menus and no Facebook page at all, so nobody can even recommend them in community groups.

Core truth: **likes and comments don't make money — actions do.** Every objective ties to revenue. But you can't sell to people who never see you, which is why the work splits unevenly:

> **70% of the effort: engage, entertain, educate. 20%: community & connection. 10%: sell & promote.**

## The 9-Post Strategy

Work in batches of nine posts (not a month-long content calendar — you're a venue operator, not an agency):

| Pillar | Posts per batch | Job |
|---|---|---|
| **Engage / Entertain / Educate (EEE)** | 4 | Teach the algorithm (and humans) that your content is interesting, so your later sells get seen |
| **Community & Connection (CC)** | 3 | Humanise the brand; build the relationship |
| **Sell / Promote** | 2 | Convert the attention into bookings/orders |

Mechanics:
- **Cadence is flexible:** spread one batch over a fortnight or a month. **3–4 purposeful posts a week beats posting daily with no purpose** — purposeless daily posting can actually penalise your account.
- **Iterate:** before writing the next nine, review the last nine. Which performed? Build the next batch from what worked.
- The pyramid is deliberately **reversed** versus what most venues do (which is 90% selling).

### Pillar 1 — Engage, Entertain, Educate (4 posts)

**Engage** — spark interaction, even mild controversy:
- "This is our **least-ordered** menu item" (swipe to reveal). Fans of the item flood in to defend it.
- Reposting that a reality-TV cast (MAFS) visited, captioned "We might not be love experts, but we are experts in good drinks and a good time." Hundreds of likes; even the negative comments came from the exact target audience (you only recognise the cast if you watch the show).
- The payoff: engagement tells Instagram/Facebook your content is interesting, so the algorithm shows your *next* (selling) post.

**Entertain** — funny, relatable, behind-the-scenes:
- A staff member slipping on a wet floor next to the wet-floor sign (owner in on the joke) → thousands of views, customers coming in to check on him, organic word of mouth.
- **Save your boo-boos and blunders** — CCTV is your best entertainment source (entire TV shows were built on home-video fails). A dropped wine box in 2017 still ranks among the best-performing posts the presenter ever ran.
- Current trend formats work: "we pray all our boss's stress goes away" (staff vanish), POV register-down dancing.

**Educate** — share the inner workings; people are obsessed with how things are made:
- "How to pour a nitro beer" — among a brewery's best-ever reels.
- How to hold chopsticks; why a steak knife cuts better; how to store smashed avo (paper towel + lime juice trick); cake-decorating timelapses; brewery tours; "what it costs to fit out a kitchen" walkthroughs ("if you've got a spare $1,000 for my fryer, chips are back tomorrow" — when people understand operating costs, they part with money to support you more willingly).
- Nothing is too small. Owner-shows-humility content crushes: a poke-bar owner pretending it's "his first day" to excuse a mistake — 197k **likes**.

### Pillar 2 — Community & Connection (3 posts)

- **Team highlights:** farewell posts (a departing staffer photoshopped into the uniform of his next career), driver's-licence wins, finishing uni, going on parental leave. **Humanise the brand — people buy from people.**
- **Customer features:** the guest who hired a private violinist for his wife on Valentine's Day; congratulating the couples whose weddings you cater ("thank you for letting us be part of your day") — social proof *and* care.
- **Local community involvement:** sponsor the player of the match and say so; free hot chips for the school excursion next door (the kids' thank-you drawings became a great post); "Hey [suburb] — we know cost of living is rough, so here are our new lunch value specials." If you do good in the community, **share it online** — almost nobody does, and it's the most connective content there is.
- **Open letters / explainers:** a pizzeria accused of shrinkflation in a local group posted an open letter explaining the trays hadn't changed and they understood times were tight → 248 likes, 26 comments, 9 community shares (shares put you in front of the exact same demographic — friends of fans).
- **Day-in-the-life / monthly wrap-ups:** an owner who vlogs the daily struggles gets hugged by customers who've never met her; a brewery's second-birthday photo dump (24 months of moments) pulled 216 likes off pure nostalgia.

### Pillar 3 — Sell & Promote (2 posts)

- **LTOs with FOMO language:** every special ends with **"available for [month] — or until sold out."** "Until sold out" manufactures the persona of a venue that sells out, which creates more hype. (The presenter checks members' posts for this phrase.)
- **Product features as upsells:** the why-acai post; "add bacon to your smashed avo — crowd favourite"; build-your-own crepe toppings; prawn topper on the steak. Show that customising *up* (adding, not swapping) is normal and delicious — it lifts ACS.
- **Events & bookings — with personality and urgency:** owner to camera: "I've just checked the bookings — only five seatings left Saturday night. Jump online now; I hate turning people away." That's how you promote bookings, not a flat "book here" graphic.
- **Presentation tricks that out-perform graphics:**
  - Don't post the promo graphic — **photograph your A-frame** with the graphic in it and post *that*. Real-world objects stop the scroll; raw graphics are saturated.
  - In-venue, **handwritten chalkboard specials get read before designed posters** every time.
  - Gift cards: a person holding one against a fun background beats a flat product shot. New menu: a team member holding it.

### Stories — your secret sauce for selling
Stories feel personal, real, behind-the-scenes — and they vanish in 24 hours, so imperfect is fine. Uses:
- **Daily humanisers:** "staff member of the day" features (customers start greeting staff on the street); "meet the crew catering this wedding"; "your bartenders this weekend."
- **Direct selling with links:** "Beautiful sunny arvo — happy hour on the deck" + a tappable **link straight to the booking page or the exact product** (Easter catering box → link to that box, not the homepage). Shorten every step.

## Posting with purpose (the process)

Before anything goes live, ask:
1. **Does this align with my brand voice?** (Cheeky brand = cheeky copy. Tell ChatGPT to be "edgy/funny/humorous" — or to "fully send it" — then dial the drafts back to taste.)
2. **What action do I want the customer to take, or how do I want them to feel?**
3. **Which business goal does it serve?** Every post maps to a goal: sell out trivia, book the weekends, fill Mother's Day.

**Saturation campaigns:** when one goal matters, every week's content feeds it. Want Mother's Day fully booked? All April content references mums — "top 5 dishes to spoil mum (taste-tested by our staff's actual mums)", booking pushes, the lot.
**Case result:** a "sip & graze" package pushed through targeted Facebook groups generated **$5,500 of bookings in 1 hour 15 minutes**. The formula: educate → entertain → engage → connect → *then tell them exactly what to do*.

### The engagement checklist (run every post through it; laminate it)
- [ ] Clear purpose?
- [ ] Which pillar — EEE, CC, or Sell?
- [ ] **Eye-catching visual or video?** Never a flat meal photo on a steel bench — stage it, have someone hold it (5 extra minutes).
- [ ] **Compelling caption?** Formula: **Hook → short story → call to action.** ("This is our least-ordered dish → its Nonna-recipe backstory → book Friday via the link in bio before it leaves the menu.") Ask ChatGPT for exactly that structure.
- [ ] **Specific CTA?** "Book your 30th package" / "Order the Easter box" — never a vague "check out our website."
- [ ] **Formatted for platform?** Instagram = **4:5 portrait** (Canva: "Instagram post portrait"); squares look wrong in the modern feed.
- [ ] **Hashtags: 5–7 per post.** One = your business name; four to six = location + discovery terms people actually search ("#brisbaneeats #brisbanefoodie #sydneyrestaurant"); one = the subject ("#margarita" on a margarita post).
- [ ] **Location tag = your suburb, never your own business.** People explore the *suburb* they're visiting or moving to; nobody browses your business's own tag. (Put your address in your bio instead.)
- [ ] **Timing:** Instagram timing barely matters — the algorithm decides when to surface posts (a reel can pop a week later). Facebook is more time-sensitive; note that plain text-only informative Facebook posts ("we're open Saturday for bread pickup") perform fine without imagery.
- [ ] **Respond to every comment.** Ignoring comments is like blanking someone who says hello on the street. Even a waving GIF counts. Reply *in brand voice* — decide your GIF/emoji/grammar style and keep it consistent (the presenter's "good ol' time" typo-isms are deliberate brand voice).

### Track what works
Your audience tells you what they like through likes. Audit your last six months of posts: a venue with five zero-engagement food posts and **54 likes on one photo of the owner at the coffee machine** has been told, loudly, to post the owner working — and kept posting food instead. Owner-on-camera consistently spikes engagement (owners pouring beers blind, riding the mechanical bull). Build a simple 7-day schedule per goal: post purpose / content / platform / goal — then check whether revenue for that goal moved. (Full metric definitions in file 07.)

### Final principles
- Content isn't about looks; it's about **connection and conversation** — the ugliest post often wins.
- No likes/comments = no trust yet. Tell more of your story before you sell.
- **Done is better than perfect. Progress over perfection** — every post just needs a purpose.

## Q&A — Content session

**Q: Nine posts every week?**
A: No — nine posts per **batch** ("section"). Spread them over a fortnight or a month. Review which of the nine performed before building the next nine. Volume without purpose can penalise you.

**Q: How do I find local TikTok/Instagram food influencers?**
A: Search "#[suburb]foodies", "#[city]eats", or "top 5 cafes [suburb]" content and see who made it and who follows them. (See also file 01.)

**Q: How do I make sure an influencer visit is worth it?**
A: **Clear directives, in writing, before the visit.** One-page agreement: deliverables (a reel, X photos, stories), and **raw footage delivered to a Google Drive you own for reuse**, signed. (A venue paid for an influencer through an agency and ended up with nothing on their feed and no footage — no agreement.) Pro workflow: send a pre-visit form of recommended items; they tick choices; print it for the chef — "table 42 is the influencer, presentation immaculate, it's being filmed."

**Q: How do I convert social attention into bums on seats?**
A: **Links + stories + telling people exactly what to do.** Weekly rhythms work: every Wednesday, a story link to book Thursday trivia; every Tuesday, the taco-day order link. And the saturation-campaign case above: $5.5k in 75 minutes once the audience was warmed and the instruction was specific.

**Q: I have multiple target markets. How do I speak to both?**
A: Split by **days** (Mon–Wed content for audience A, Thu–Sun for audience B) or by **platform** (Instagram speaks to one, Facebook to the other).

**Q: Recap the hashtag rules?**
A: 5–7 per post: 1 business name + 4–6 location/discovery tags + 1 post-subject tag.

**Q: We're sponsoring a community ride (Orange Week bike day). What's the carrot to get riders in?**
A: Serve the *moment*: recovery smoothies post-ride, double-espresso "pre-workout" before, "supporting a rider? wait here in the aircon", and a tie-in donation ("$1 from every carrot cake to the fundraiser").

**Q: My niche (e.g. deer-hunting community) might offend other followers. Post it anyway?**
A: If they're your target audience — yes. **The opinions of people who don't pay your bills don't matter.** The presenter's bright-pink cafe with crude cups was told "we don't need this city trash in our small town" — and thrives, because the people it *does* speak to show up. Trolls who never buy a coffee don't get a vote. (Pair with the ideal-guest principle in file 03.)
