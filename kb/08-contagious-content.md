## Why people watch content

The session opened with an experiment first run at retreat: a video compilation played to a room of ~70 with no instruction — and the whole room went quiet and watched. The point: **without instruction, you can grab attention by creating content people want to consume.** Every clip in that compilation had gone viral (hundreds of thousands of views), and a percentage of any local audience watching that kind of content is in your foot-traffic area.

Why do we watch — even content with nothing to do with us? Curiosity, escapism, the dopamine hit, music, seeing experts being awesome, behind the scenes, switching off. We've all lost an hour doom-scrolling. The brands everyone recognises from a logo alone (McDonald's, Domino's and friends) got there because **they know exactly who they're talking to and who they're producing content for.** That's the job at local scale: don't produce content for yourself — produce it for the people you want coming through the door.

## The interests-based customer avatar ("what is Lucy watching?")

Demographics alone aren't the avatar. "Lucy, 25, single mum, dentist" tells you nothing about what to create. The question is: **what is Lucy watching?** Is she on Netflix? Love Island? A Blockhead? What does she read, what's she listening to, what does she do — morning runs, cycling, lycra?

When you know their interests, you can create memes and content around the things they already love. They think "this café has the same humour as me" and start associating your brand with things they like — the same mechanism that has people wearing luxury labels to associate themselves with a class of brand.

**Worked example (presenter's cafe, dated):** during Taylor Swift's Australian tour, the presenter switched all the cold-cup stickers in her cafe to Swift lyrics and references — deliberately targeting her Gen Z avatar (she's a millennial; the stickers weren't for her). Over the following two-to-three weeks she saw roughly a 38% lift (*as transcribed — treat the number as indicative*), with people driving across Brisbane for an iced latte with a Taylor Swift sticker. **Speak to their interests and they feel seen.**

Rules and actions:
- **You can't produce content for everybody.** Trying to is how owners burn out (or end up needing an agency). Decide who you want *more* of — if your crowd is 40–55 and you want 20–30s, go fishing for 20–30s.
- If you don't know what your avatar is into, **find a real person who is one** — a niece or nephew, a mate's kid, a team member who fits the avatar — write down their name and pick their brain about what they watch, read and listen to. Owners often don't know what's current (no time for TV, calls in the car, licensed music in-house); borrow someone who does.
- Different avatars can live on different platforms — see the Q&A below.

## The brand voice worksheet

Do people look at your account and know who you are? Usually not. The fix is a circle-the-options worksheet (in the class workbook) that pins your voice down:

- **We or I?** Is the account about what *you're* building, or what *we* are doing?
- Funny / humorous / quirky / posh / serious / formal / informal
- Slang, proper grammar, acronyms? Direct or indirect?
- Bright / monochrome / eclectic / retro — what style is your venue?
- Personal or impersonal?
- **Political, controversial, or Switzerland?** You'll face this every year: Australia Day, Melbourne Cup. Run with it and you speak directly to customers who want to get glitzed up and have a punt — but may exclude others (e.g. people focused on animal welfare). Avoid it and you trade as usual. Decide deliberately; don't drift into it.
- Formats: stories, photos, videos, reels, carousels — circle as many as you'll actually use; it feeds the 9-post planning.
- Entertaining, educational, emotional — you can be all three.

**Brand-voice case (dated example):** McDonald's built an entire voice around Grimace — terrible punctuation, direct, humorous ("hi friends, my shakes are now available across all stores, I think we have five"). The plan wasn't to sell from those posts; it was to spike engagement so that **when they later sell other offers, the audience is already engaged and the organic touchpoints exist.** That's the same logic as the 9-post pyramid.

### Q: Gut feel, or hours of deliberation on the worksheet?
A: **Gut feel to start.** Overthinking it leads to perfection paralysis. Your content will evolve: if the funny posts your team throws up get the comments, the likes, and a little revenue spike that week, you've just been told what your audience wants. Keep trickling in the educational ones, but let people guide you. Start; refine from response.

## Storytelling is the secret weapon

Competitors can copy your content style; they can't copy your story. **Your brand is you — and if it's not, it's disingenuous, and customers can smell that from a mile away.**

- **The reframe:** members who heard a former McDonald's Australia leader speak at retreat about carrying the brand through years of enormous debt now drive past the golden arches and see resilience instead of nuggets. One shared story permanently changed how they see the brand. That's the effect your story can have locally.
- **The presenter's opening-night story:** at 1am before opening day, the hot water system blew and flooded the cafe and hallway. They mopped through the night, a supplier plumbed a new system in at dawn, and they opened on time. She shared the story that night — **day two traded double day one**, because people came in invested ("I can't believe you went to bed at 2am and opened at 6"). A year on, customers still ask how the hot water system's going.
- **Disaster-recovery stories out-engage success stories.** We're wired for it (it's why news works the way it does). Burst pipes, the car through the shopfront, the Christmas-menu test that tasted rotten — share the recovery.
- **Team stories count too:** the school leaver skipping schoolies who gets a public shout-out (and a surprise celebration), apprentices finishing their time, birthdays, first cars. It creates engagement *and* shows you genuinely care about your people — because you do.
- **Map your story:** scribble three to five real things — where you came from, corporate-to-hospitality, kids, a divorce you worked through, the dog. These are the things people resonate with.
- **The burnout antidote (coach's observation from the session):** if you're out of love with the business, this exercise forces you to look at it with interested eyes. The mundane day-to-day contains stories — but only if you're looking for them, and only if you ask your team what's going on in their lives.

## The four elements of contagious content

1. **Evoke emotion and awe** — the hot water story made people feel something; that feeling becomes investment in your business and your people.
2. **Humour and relatability** — equipment always dies at 5pm Friday, never Monday; fridges run warm right after the stock delivery; the sickie on your biggest trading day. If they can't relate, let them laugh.
3. **Educational and valuable** — supplier and produce stories: where the fish comes from, what goes into grass-fed beef before it hits the plate, that you support Australian farmers, how it's made, who makes it, how long you've made it for, where the recipe came from. When people understand what goes into it, they value it (and pay for it) more willingly.
4. **Entertaining and unexpected** — the dropped tray of glasses works because you expected him to walk it to the table. Unexpected openings are **hooks**.

## Pirates: lookalike competitor analysis and the content vault

Marketing competitor analysis is **not** about Joe Blow down the street (that's the financial kind). Find businesses in your exact niche **anywhere in the world** who are smashing it:

1. In your workbook, record: their name, are they on Facebook / Instagram / TikTok / elsewhere, and are they actually getting results there?
2. Search your own category on Instagram (fish and chips, pizza, burgers). If a lookalike business is on your Explore page, Instagram has already told you their content works — so it'll pretty much work for you.
3. Save every piece of theirs that earned big engagement, and **recreate it yourself.** It won't be identical — different team, different room, your story — and that's the point. "We're pirates."
4. **Build a content vault:** use the save/collections feature on Instagram, Facebook and TikTok; create collaborative folders and add the team member who'll be making your content (the how-to resource lives in Elite Mastermind). The vault means you always know what to make next and what to cross-post.

(Pinterest is good for graphics and image inspiration; less so for video.)

## The 9-post concept (as taught in this session)

Produce content **nine pieces at a time**. More than nine gets overwhelming and purposeless; fewer and you lose momentum. Think of it as fishing:

| Layer | Posts | Job |
|---|---|---|
| Entertain / engage / educate | 4 | **The bait** — make them laugh, make them smarter, hook them; this is what draws people to your profile |
| Community & connection | 3 | Your story, your team, giving back — now they're invested |
| Sell & promote | 2 | Now you've earned the right to offer them something |

- Staggering is fine — two engage, one sell, two community, two engage, one promote. The ratio is what matters, not the order.
- **Stories are not part of the nine.** Stories are daily, real-time, behind-the-scenes — e.g. the presenter's cafe posts a "barista of the day" every day (it removes the fear of the unknown and lets customers build favourites), and a "this is your weekend team" post with names works the same way.
- Sell-post ideas: a weekend hash-brown add-on, a free small-to-medium coffee upgrade — **and get your supplier to fund the difference.** Suppliers will happily join a monthly special that pushes more of their product through your venue.
- *Cadence note:* this session describes executing nine posts weekly for fastest organic growth; the 2025 series (file 06) softens that to one batch spread over a fortnight or a month, with 3–4 purposeful posts a week beating purposeless daily posting. **Treat file 06 as the current cadence guidance** — both agree purpose beats volume.

## Sell through community proof

- **The $2 hot chips story (a member's seafood shop, genericised):** a kindergarten group touring next door got a free serve of hot chips — five minutes' work, a couple of dollars of cost. Weeks later a hand-drawn thank-you painting arrived, the kindy posted it everywhere, and mums kept coming in for weeks. Share moments like that: drawings, thank-you notes, even a shout-out to the reliable delivery drivers who help your business run.
- **The 16-carton story (a member's brewery, genericised):** a school fundraiser asked for a couple of cartons; the brewery donated all sixteen (~$20 cost each). The auction helped raise **$22,000**, extending the school's reading-assistance program from years 1–2 to years 1–6. Share that — it also flips the negative association around alcohol by tying what you make to community good. The presenter would even put ad spend behind a story like that.
- **Pitch your good news to local media.** Local magazines, news outlets and papers are always hunting for positive stories to sprinkle between the grim ones. When you win an award or make an impact, reach out — nine times out of ten you'll get a yes, and it costs nothing but the time to engage.

## Consistency: who, where, when, what, how — then monitor

Marketing is shiny-object syndrome territory: the excitement fades when other tasks pile up. Lock the system in the workbook:

- **Who is responsible?** Not just you — you wear too many hats. A trusted team member, the team collectively, an agency, or UGC (customer-created content). Your youngsters are great with their phones and will feel trusted with the responsibility; people naturally want to impress.
- **Where does raw content get shared?** An Instagram group chat, WhatsApp, a business Facebook group, or a collaborative Google Drive the team drops footage into for editing and posting.
- **When — set an achievable frequency.** Hard mode (a post a day for 30 days to see what happens) or a minimum of three a week. Achievable targets keep you motivated; momentum compounds.
- **What:** food, team members, customers willing to be on camera, and the venue itself — walkthrough videos build brand familiarity because people are nosy and want to scope the room before they visit.
- Bookkeeping aside from the session: food produced for content/photos should be recorded as marketing rather than ordinary wastage (*presenter's understanding via her bookkeeper, as transcribed — confirm with your own bookkeeper*).
- **How:** reels, carousels, posts. Carousel templates make repeatables easy: this week's top-selling beers, the week's favourite pizzas, your top five regulars (grab a selfie with them).
- **Monitor — your analytics are a gold mine.** Instagram, Facebook and TikTok literally tell you which content your customers loved. Check weekly (Sunday night in bed counts) or monthly: drop the best performers into the team chat and ask "how do we recreate this?" **You don't need to reinvent the wheel — replicate what's already working.** (Metric definitions → file 07.)

A member who delegated content after retreat reported the biggest unlock was *defining the format*: no more static single-image posts — everything is a carousel, a reel, or a story.

### Why static posts under-perform (engagement mechanics as taught)
- A reel watched for 3+ seconds tells the platform you make content your customers want — your next post gets pushed further.
- The moment someone swipes a carousel, they've *engaged*, even without liking it.
- That engagement compounds: your stories surface earlier, your next post ranks higher — which is exactly why the bait posts exist, so your sell posts and story links actually get seen.

## Q&A — Contagious Content session

**Q: We're an aquarium *and* a restaurant — families versus fine diners. Do I just pick one avatar?**
A: Target the families first, and not only for the season — **target whoever organically produces the most word of mouth.** A family visit cascades: the kids tell show-and-tell, classmates ask their parents to go, dad tells the workplace on Monday, mum tells the daycare drop-off. One visit, four audiences. (The presenter targets Gen Z and makes every in-venue touchpoint shareable so her word of mouth runs through their socials.) For the fine diners, build a separate path: an upmarket in-house incentive that grows an email list, with emails written to *that* avatar. And split platforms by avatar — you'd never target 60-year-olds on TikTok, but on Facebook, 110%.

**Q: My agency says only two static posts a week but a reel every day we trade. Can you post too much?**
A: **You can post too much if the posts have no purpose.** Purposeless posting reads as spamming the algorithm: no engagement, no likes, no comments means your next post gets pushed out less. A daily reel is fantastic *if* each one earns engagement and views — not if it's just pictures strung together. Don't post rubbish; it works against you.

**Q: How do I make a carousel?**
A: On Instagram, tap the + then the stacked-squares icon ("share more"), select up to 20 photos (*limit as at recording*), and post. The killer use: the **weekly photo dump** — ten candid shots of the week (the water leak, a reshared customer story, the Christmas-menu tasting that flopped). It's the modern version of the nightclub photographer: people go looking for themselves, and engagement follows.
