## What is a brand?

Your brand is **not** your business. Your brand is **how people feel and what they perceive** when they encounter you — when they leave your venue, see your content, drive past your shopfront, or open one of your takeaway boxes.

Why it matters: a strong, cohesive brand builds **awareness**, which creates **organic word of mouth** — your cheapest source of new customers. It also builds trust and loyalty, differentiates you from competitors, and means people recognise your posts as *yours* without reading the name.

Benchmark examples: McDonald's = the Golden Arches, convenience, drive-through speed. Subway = "eat fresh". KFC = the Colonel. Domino's = value and cheap Tuesday pizzas. You recognise them from colours, fonts and assets alone — that's the goal at local scale.

### Where your branding lives (audit this list)
Menus, website, socials, packaging and takeaway boxes, coffee cups, napkins, greaseproof paper, pizza boxes, uniforms and hats, business cards, A-frames, storefront signage, chairs, vehicles, newspaper ads, merch, employment packs, function/catering contracts. **Action step:** write a list of every single place your business appears and check each item is on-brand. Easy win: if your team all wear random hats, order branded ones — staff wearing them to the shops after work is free brand awareness in your community.

**Exercise from the session:** find five businesses in your niche (breweries, bakeries, etc.) anywhere in the world with strong branding. Study what they do, then recreate the principles.

## The Brand Positioning Statement (BPS)

A BPS is simply a paragraph that differentiates you from everyone else in your industry. A good BPS answers four things:

1. **Who are you?** (e.g. husband-and-wife owned; the community OG that's served the suburb for 20 years; a fun young crew)
2. **What do you offer/serve?** (not just food — convenience, memories, celebrations, an experience)
3. **Who do you serve?** (your community, a city, anyone celebrating life milestones)
4. **What makes you different?** (your X-factor: heritage building, longevity, signature item, sourcing)

Key principles:
- It's **aspirational, not historical** — state who you intend to be ("the number one gelato bar in Byron Bay, known for..."), not just what you currently are.
- It can be **updated any time** — when your offer changes, when you hit a milestone, whenever.
- Write it **in your brand voice**. Quirky brand = quirky statement ("even the Karen of all Karens would be impressed"). French patisserie = throw in a French phrase. Mexican = open with "Hola". A caravan park nailed it with: *"You don't visit, you belong."*
- Don't hide the humans. A remote husband-and-wife owner worried "owned by a married couple" was misleading since they're not always on site — keep it in. It tells people this is a family business, not a corporation with an HR department; that builds trust, especially for catering and functions.
- Corporate, cold language kills conversion. People want authenticity.

### Big-brand examples (structure to copy)
- **McDonald's:** "a leader in the fast food industry with quick, friendly service and consistency across thousands of convenient locations… dedication to improving operations and customer satisfaction sets it apart." → positions as leader, names the category, claims convenience/friendliness/consistency, separates from competitors.
- **Coca-Cola:** "For quality beverage seekers, Coca-Cola offers the most refreshing options… inspires happiness and makes a positive difference in customers' lives." → names who it serves, the experience, and the emotional association (happiness, memories).

### The ChatGPT prompt (use this verbatim)
> "I want to write a brand positioning statement for my business. Ask me 10 questions about my business that will help you build a strong brand positioning statement in my brand voice."

Answer the 10 questions; it drafts the BPS; you finesse it. You don't need to be a word wizard — you run a venue.

### Where the BPS gets used
- Google Business Profile "about" (shortened to one sentence — see file 05)
- Facebook and Instagram bios (shortened via ChatGPT — see file 04)
- Middle of your website homepage (a synopsis of who you are)
- Employment packs (so new hires know who you are)
- Think of it as your elevator pitch with purpose.

### Brand voice
Defined alongside the BPS: Are you a "we" or an "I"? Funny, political, controversial, entertaining, educational? Slang or proper grammar? This voice must run through the BPS, every caption, and even how you reply to comments. (Members who've done the "Contagious Content" training have a brand-voice worksheet — reuse it.)

## Brand assets

Built off your BPS. Four components:

### 1. Logo suite (yes, logos — plural)
One logo isn't enough. Build a suite:
- **Primary logo** — the full mark.
- **Sub-logos** — simplified elements pulled from it (a graphic, a monogram) used on greaseproof paper, contract footers, watermarks on employment packs, story overlays. Think Heineken's red star appearing at the bottom of every post.
- **Favicon** — the tiniest version (often just initials, like a big "D!" or "FC") used in browser tabs and as the simplest profile mark. In a world of 20–50 open tabs, an unreadable favicon = invisible brand.

If your current logo is detailed/wordy, either break it down yourself or ask your original designer for a suite.

### 2. Colours — choose four to six
Big brands run two colours; as a small business *learning* design, give yourself 4–6 for flexibility, then narrow over time based on what performs (which A-frame promo worked? which cup do customers photograph and tag?).

**No palette yet? Pinterest + Canva method:** search Pinterest for "[your main colour] colour palette", save a board image you love, upload it to Canva, use the colour dropper to extract the exact hex codes, and save them to your Canva Brand Kit. From then on, every template you use can be switched to brand colours in one click — halving design time and making everything instantly recognisable.

**Colour psychology for hospitality:**
- **Red increases hunger** — that's why fast-food giants use it.
- **Blue is a natural appetite suppressant** (it's why weight-loss brands use it). If blue is core to your identity (e.g. Greek venues), do what Greek brands do: white as the primary, blue as the accent — don't go fully blue.
- **Pink skews feminine** (mums, teen girls) as a primary — but it isn't a rule: a bright-pink cafe with yellow floors reports ~40% of its patronage is tradies in work boots. Your real audience decides.
- Green/yellow/orange/red together read gender-neutral.
- The test that matters: do the people actually buying from you love it enough to photograph and share it?

Why consistency matters: scrolling a feed of food posts, people identify a venue by colour and font before they read the name. If every post is a different style, even your own followers scroll past without realising it's you.

### 3. Fonts — a suite of two to three
- **Primary font** — headlines, menu item names (often matches your logo).
- **Secondary font** — body text, menu descriptions.
- **Decorative font** — accents only: "NEW", "GF", a highlight on your most profitable menu item, a punchy quote on a tile. Never paragraphs.
Match the font personality to the brand: punchy, country/rustic, cursive Parisian, fun handwritten. Stuck? Google "font suites" / "Canva font combinations" (e.g. "French font suite", "playful font suite"). Also decide capitalisation rules — all-lowercase can read friendly and approachable; ALL CAPS is impactful in small doses.

### 4. Graphics — six to eight recurring styles
Recurring visual elements associated with your brand: sprinkles for a dessert brand, melting ice cream, stencil pizza outlines, Day-of-the-Dead skulls for a Mexican venue, stars/galaxy motifs for a "magic"-themed brewery. Decide the *style* too — 3D render (think Cadbury's chocolate-toned 3D look), flat 2D, hand-sketched, monochrome, cartoon/kawaii. Canva's AI can generate options in a chosen style.

**Real example of assets working without a logo:** a cafe's uniform is just its iced-latte-cup sub-logo on the front and "CAFFEINE DEALER" in brand font/colours on the back — no business logo anywhere, yet everyone knows whose shirt it is. That's the bar: recognisable with the name removed.

### ChatGPT shortcut for the whole kit
Upload your logo + a Pinterest palette screenshot + a screenshot of Canva fonts you like into one ChatGPT chat and ask it to create your **brand asset guide**. Don't make it harder than it needs to be.

## Pace yourself (and a warning)

- Rebrand in **small increments**: first make socials, website, and Google use the same logo, colours and fonts (cheap/free). Swap uniforms when you're due to reorder anyway. Done is better than perfect; almost-done beats paralysed-by-perfection.
- **Don't out-market your operations.** Before driving brand awareness hard, make sure COGS are right, the team can deliver at higher capacity, and function packages have solid contracts. Marketing amplifies whatever's underneath it.

## Q&A — Brand session

**Q: I feel like I have to change everything — I'm overwhelmed and not creative.**
A: Completely normal. Create 2–3 concepts (or have a designer do it), then test them with friends, family, and ideally people in your target market — pick the favourite. You don't need to change everything at once; start with matching socials + website + Google, then chip away.

**Q: Is professional branding expensive?**
A: It ranges from a few hundred dollars on Fiverr to $20k+ agency packages. Cheaper routes: Fiverr, a local graphic-design student (ask in your community Facebook page), or do-it-yourself with the Pinterest/Canva/ChatGPT methods above. (Delish Digital clients get brand assets built within their strategy.)

**Q: Thoughts on green + hot pink + yellow + orange + red + white together?**
A: Depends which is the primary and whether it speaks to your audience. Hot pink as a primary skews to women/teen girls (fine if that's the target); as an accent it's flexible. Green/yellow/orange/red is gender-neutral. And remember the pink-cafe-full-of-tradies counterexample — watch who actually buys.

**Q: Cost-effective uniforms?**
A: Options: bulk plain tees from Kmart/Big W + **UV DTF transfers** (upload your design, they print it, you iron it on with a tea towel — great for seasonal designs like Valentine's/Christmas/Halloween shirts); Etsy suppliers; local wholesale shirt makers; or ask in the members' group for supplier recommendations. Bonus plays: ask your **coffee supplier to co-fund uniforms** carrying both logos; and put your wins on shirts — "Australia's #3 beer release" or "Best cookies in [town]" turns staff into walking billboards.

**Q: Where do I get coffee-cup stickers?**
A: Local print shops (e.g. Minuteman Press in Brisbane was used at roughly $11/sheet — *as transcribed, verify pricing*), or print them yourself for small runs. Ask the members' group for bulk suppliers.

**Q: We're rebranding and changing fonts after six years — will it hurt recognition?**
A: No. Owners forget the population churns constantly: people move in and out of the suburb, school leavers start buying their own breakfasts, fresh 18-year-olds walk into licensed venues, new mums are hunting for a first outing with the pram. There's always a wave of first-timers for whom your "new" brand is simply *the* brand. Establish who you want to be seen as.
