# Beyond the Punch Card — Building a Profitable Loyalty Program

**Module:** Beyond the Punch Card (loyalty programs)
**Core question it answers:** How do I turn one-off customers into repeat regulars with a loyalty program that protects my margins?

A loyalty program is not about giving away free food. It is a strategic tool to build relationships and capture data that drives long-term profit. The goal: win a customer once, then retain them with effective marketing so they keep coming back.

## Why retention beats acquisition

- A 5% lift in customer retention can lift profit by as much as 100% (a figure the class draws from retention research and the major point-of-sale providers). Retention is where profit lives.
- Chasing new customers eats profit every time, through agency or ad spend, buy-one-get-one discounts, and delivery-marketplace commissions. Once a customer has visited and joined your program, the cost to bring them back is close to zero.
- Loyal customers visit more often, spend more per visit, and become advocates who recommend you to friends.

## Choose the right program structure

There is no one-size-fits-all. A café with no liquor, a late bar, and a 5am bakery all serve different avatars, so the structure has to suit your customer and their behaviour. The main options:

- **Points-based.** Earn points per dollar spent (think Starbucks, Domino's, airline and supermarket schemes). Easy to understand, easy to track progress, and it lifts average spend, not just visit frequency, because customers consolidate their spending with you instead of spreading it across competitors. Members are already trained on this in daily life (frequent-flyer points, supermarket rewards, card points), so moving it digital does not create friction.
- **Tiered / status.** Silver, gold, platinum levels (airline status, beauty-store tiers, large fast-food rewards). Status creates a barrier to exit: once someone reaches a tier they keep spending to avoid being demoted. Gamify it for your venue, for example a regular who attends enough trivia or poker nights unlocks a perk (half-price pizza, a complimentary drink) and resets if they miss one.
- **Subscription (paid perks).** A weekly or monthly fee that unlocks instant perks. It guarantees cash flow and locks the customer in. Examples: an unlimited-coffee subscription that brings someone in daily (they add a bagel or wrap while they are there because the coffee feels "free"); a low monthly fee that gives a complimentary side every order so they choose you over the competition. The old paper punch card still suits high-frequency, low-ticket venues, but move it digital, paper cards get lost, can be gamed, and capture zero data.
- **Gamified.** Make it fun to drive frequency and upsells: scratch-and-win cards (spend a threshold, scratch, scan a QR, details load into your point of sale with the reward), or game mechanics tied to a larger size (a game card only on medium and large meals nudges people up from a small). Gamification taps the natural desire for achievement and competition, so people pay attention and spend more often.

## Integrate with your point of sale (non-negotiable)

Decide on your point of sale, commit to it, then set the loyalty program up properly the first time. Use a loyalty system that integrates natively with that point of sale so it works across in-store, QR table ordering, online ordering, and email in one place (examples named in the class include Square Loyalty, and Lightspeed with Marsello). Ask your provider for onboarding help, they have people to set it up and support you.

Why it matters: points apply instantly, staff do not juggle separate tablets, and the experience stays seamless. When systems do not talk to each other, a customer spends money but cannot claim points, and walks out annoyed. You cannot run a modern loyalty program separately from your operations.

Capture the data that matters: name, birthday, and order history. Order history is the asset that makes everything below work.

## Push loyalty toward online and app ordering

If your average spend online or via delivery is higher than in store, add online-only incentives (for example, order through the app and your first coffee is on us). Pushing loyalty to the app or website trains customers to order digitally, which shortens queues, saves labour, and lifts average order value because automated upsells fire every time. When the venue gets busy, staff upselling is the first thing to drop; built-in online upsells never get missed.

## Set it up well: three non-negotiables

- **Simplify the sign-up.** Minimal fields, a first name and an email or phone number, maybe a dietary preference. Long forms kill participation; if it feels like a mortgage application, people will not join. Capture more over time.
- **Personalise with data.** If the program is integrated with your point of sale you already hold order history, so you can tailor offers. Do not email a vegetarian a discount on chicken wings. "You are one veggie burger away from a free one" converts far better than a generic promotion. Birthday programs work well: a complimentary birthday meal brings the regular in with guests, at the cost of one meal at normal food-cost.
- **Optimise the rewards.** Test a reward, keep it if it works, drop it if it does not, and revisit later. Do not flog a dead horse.

## Design rewards: high perceived value, low cost

Choose rewards that feel generous to the guest but cost you little. Comp a tangible item at low cost of goods (a coffee, garlic bread) rather than dollars off. A "$5 off" comes straight off your bottom line; a complimentary item only costs you its cost of goods (often around a fifth of the menu price). Frame it as "one on us", not "free", it is you giving it to them.

The community rule from this class: no more dollar-off discounts, comp a tangible item so you protect your margins.

Make rewards micro and frequently attainable, not a distant $500 threshold, so people stay engaged and you keep capturing data. You can exclude your tightest-margin dishes from redemption, or flag only the items that can be redeemed.

## Market and manage it, or it fails

You can build the best program in the world, but if nobody knows it exists it will fail. It needs active promotion and consistent management across every touchpoint.

- **Staff advocacy.** Train the frontline to mention and sign people up as part of the sequence of service ("have you joined our rewards program? Spend a bit more tonight and take home a free brownie"). A function or party can enrol dozens at once.
- **Omni-channel reminders.** Automated emails about a point balance ("you are 50 points away from a free taco") trigger completion bias, people act to finish what they started. Promote on Instagram, Facebook, and TikTok.
- **Exclusivity.** Members get early access to new menu items, beer releases, or taste-testing.
- **Scarcity and urgency.** Points should expire. "Five days left", "your points expire in two days" drives dormant customers back in. Points are earned by spending, not bought, so an expiry is fair and it is a strong psychological trigger.

## Budget

The class's guidance: aim to put around 5% of annual revenue toward marketing overall. If you are going deep on a loyalty program, roughly 2% of revenue toward the loyalty initiatives and giveaways, and about 3% toward actively marketing it and acquiring new customers.

## Common pitfalls to avoid

- **Complexity.** If a customer needs a calculator to work out a reward, you have lost them. Keep it simple ("buy ten, get one on us").
- **Rewards that lack value.** Thresholds set too high make people disengage. Keep rewards small and attainable.
- **The app-only gap.** Focusing only on app users ignores the roughly 97% who dine but never download. Keep QR codes on tables that go straight to online ordering with the loyalty program built in, so you still capture casual diners and nurture them into members.

## Key takeaways

Go digital so you can track customer data. Connect the program to your point of sale and lean on your provider to set it up and support you. Personalise offers from order history. Then market it relentlessly with automations (point-balance nudges, birthday reminders, push notifications) so it stays top of mind.
