## Why define an ideal guest

You cannot market to everyone, and you can't be everything to everybody. Defining the ideal guest is the difference between a post getting 1–2 likes and a post getting hundreds — between your community scrolling past and your community feeling *heard*.

This is about who you **want to attract more of**, not necessarily who currently walks in. If you keep getting customers who "didn't read the venue" and complain about your vibe, that's a signal your messaging is attracting the wrong people. Example: a cheeky cafe prints slogans like "Oops, woke up sexy as hell again" on its cups. Some customers object — and that's fine; there's another cafe 400 m away with plain cups. The brand deliberately speaks to humorous customers and accepts repelling the rest. (See also "don't fear offending" in file 06.)

The work happens in three steps: **demographics → psychographics → challenges & offers.** Everything you post for the following weeks refers back to this avatar.

## Step 1 — Demographics

Demographics are the quantifiable characteristics of your target group: **age, sex/gender, employment, income, relationship status, dependents, location.**

### The decision-maker principle (most important idea in this module)
Your target audience is never a vague group like "families" or "corporate". It's **the decision maker inside that group**:
- "Families" → the parent (often mum) deciding where the family eats or where the function is booked.
- "Corporate catering" → the office manager or events manager placing the order. Research them properly: check Seek listings to learn the typical gender and age of office/events managers in your area, then target *that person*.

### How each demographic shapes strategy
- **Age:** target by bracket or generation (millennials, Gen Z, baby boomers) — it drives platform choice and references.
- **Employment/income:** premium product? You need employed people / business owners / CEOs with disposable income who *value* a great wood-fired pizza over a cheap one.
- **Relationship status:** want higher ACS? Target people in relationships — two people at breakfast beats one.
- **Dependents:** if your kids menu is profitable, target the married mum with three kids; ACS rises with the table size.
- **Location:** locals vs tourists changes everything — holiday-destination venues should produce location-based content.

Worked example from the session (a cocktail bar): 25–55, ~65% female, higher socio-economic professionals/business owners or spouses of big earners, married/de facto, kids, local. That level of specificity is the goal.

## Step 2 — Psychographics

Psychographics are the psychological attributes driving purchase decisions — **values, attitudes, interests, lifestyle** — where demographics are the measurable shell. (No simpler word exists; congratulations, you're officially doing real marketing.)

### Values
What matters most to them. Businesses that visibly align with customer values build stronger connection:
- **Sustainability / environmentalism** — and *say it out loud* (see Earth Hour tactic in file 01).
- **Affordability vs quality.**
- **Community support** — sponsoring the local soccer team, donating beer cases to school fundraisers, donating packaging plastics to women's shelters. If you give back, shout it from the rooftops; people who value community will connect with you.
- **Family/ownership story** — husband-and-wife or mum-and-son owned? Share the everyday struggles and wins; it's part of the brand.

### Morals
Family-orientated? Religious or spiritual? Inclusive? Hard-line on sustainability? Transparency about your own practices and morals builds stronger relationships.

### Interests (the fun one — and direct content fuel)
Music (who'd go to the Luke Combs concert vs Drake?), movies (action / documentary / horror), **which social platforms they use and how much screen time**, reading (a member sold out an event built around the *Onyx Storm* book launch by targeting BookTok readers), fitness, cooking (could you run classes?), travel, gaming, DIY, fashion, art.

### Shopping preferences
Online vs in-store; discount-driven vs luxury/social-proof driven.

### Lifestyle
Busy professional; stay-at-home parent who is the household decision maker; student; digital nomad; foodie influencer; fitness enthusiast; social butterfly; budget-conscious shopper. Lifestyle reveals daily habits and routines — which leads directly to their challenges.

**Tip:** if you don't know what (say) a 45-year-old full-time-employed parent is into, ask people in that demographic — fellow members, your staff, family. Be specific now, because you'll write actual posts from this.

## Step 3 — Challenges & offers (where the magic happens)

Identify the challenges your ideal guest faces, then **answer the challenge with a solution in your venue — and post about it.** Answer their questions before they have to ask. Worked examples:

| Challenge | Solutions to build & promote |
|---|---|
| **Fussy eaters** (kids *and* adults — the no-mushroom friend, the plain-eating husband) | Multiple kids options; happily swap sauces; "kids meal — 5 min delay from pass" button on the till with a timer so the child's food lands warm when the parents' does. Post that you understand. |
| **Time-poor** (busy professionals) | 10 grab-and-go options ready in under 10 minutes; print realistic times on food tags and menus ("Ham & cheese croissant — cold now, warmed in 2 min"). They'll grab food *and* coffee because you've answered "will this make me late?" |
| **Tradies / dirty boots** | Kick mats and boot brushes at the door, "Tradies always welcome" sign, "we mop every night — everyone's welcome, we clean for a reason." |
| **Money-conscious** | "Lunch under $15" range — keep it profitable via smaller portions and several options; cheaper mains often pull a dessert add-on, lifting ACS anyway. |
| **Parents with tired kids** | Car-park pickup: order online, food brought to the car — no dragging exhausted kids inside. Convenience converts. |

More challenge prompts from the session: Do people know **where to park**? Are you **fully air-conditioned** (say so!)? Do you allow **BYO straws** for big milkshakes (paper straws die in 5 minutes)? Have you showcased your **vegetarian** range or **dairy-free swaps** for the lactose intolerant?

### Manage expectations proactively (a challenge-solution in itself)
- Train cashiers to say upfront: "Just so you know, there's currently a 30-minute wait on kitchen food."
- Post it: "Saturdays get crazy here — please give us the grace of an extra 10 minutes on breakfast." Setting the precedent = happier customers who feel you're honest with them.

### The exercise
Write **5 challenges** your ideal guest faces, then **2 content ideas per challenge** (e.g. dirty boots → photo of the new kick mat; "we sweep & mop nightly" post). This becomes a content bank for file 06.

### The avatar template
A workbook template summarises demographics + psychographics + challenges/solutions into one **ideal guest avatar** — hand it to any agency, marketing manager, or in-house team member so everyone targets the same person. You can run multiple avatars (e.g. weekday vs weekend audiences).

## Q&A — Ideal guest session

**Q: Weekdays my crowd is office workers, tradies and government staff; weekends it's families and tourists. Should I add a kids menu just for weekends?**
A: From a marketing perspective, yes — a kids menu tells parents "you're welcome and we get it" (one slice of raisin toast, unseasoned scrambled eggs, sauce on the side). Start **weekend-only**: if it runs all week, adults sneak the cheaper small portions and your weekday ACS drops. Use small-portion versions of things you already cook. (Check the operational side — costing, kitchen flow — with your business coach; this answer is the marketing lens.)

**Q: We want to be kid-friendly but kids running wild annoys our older regulars. How do we manage both?**
A: Give kids a *contained* reason to stay put: colouring pages and paper crowns on clipboards; a small craft station (one venue let kids decorate Christmas baubles); a play corner behind a baby gate (one cafe built a mini pretend-cafe inside one — kids wouldn't leave, and mums boomed); a $50 marketplace TV + Chromecast playing Octonauts in a quiet corner; or the two-room model (one venue runs a kids/parents room and an adults-only room — everyone's happier). Safety note: if staff supervise a play area, ensure they hold the required working-with-children checks (Blue Cards in QLD). Protect the business first.

**Q: I ran a speed-dating event and posted it on socials and a poster, but bookings are dead. Pivot or push?**
A: **Facebook groups are dynamite for any specialised event** — far more than your own page. Go where the target already hangs out:
- For women: local singles groups and the "Sis, is this your man?"-style groups (full of actively dating women).
- For men (usually the harder half to fill): fishing groups, local footy club groups, State of Origin groups — wherever blokes congregate locally. You need even numbers of men and women.
- Post the event there (check each group's advertising rules). The moment one person comments "I'm so going" or tags a mate, social proof snowballs registrations — people follow their peers.

**Case study — the power of targeting interest groups:** During COVID, a city bar that had lost its foot traffic created "Last Meal", a Halloween-themed three-course dinner ($195/head) — haunted-mansion theatre, prison-guard servers, coffin-shaped brownies. It was promoted almost entirely in South-East Queensland Halloween-lover and horror-fan Facebook groups (chosen because the local theme park had cancelled its Fright Nights, leaving that audience hungry). Goal: 100 tickets. Result: sold out 100 two weeks early, waitlist hit 40, opened a second night and sold 75 more — **175 tickets at $195** from one interest-targeted campaign. Replicate the method: find the interest, find the group it lives in, build the event for them, post where they already are.

**Q: Any quick win using customer interests?**
A: The "date night in" tactic: when a movie your demographic loves drops on streaming (the session example was *The Gorge* on Apple TV+ landing on Valentine's Day), post about it and pair it with your takeaway: "Kids in bed? Movie night sorted — order your pizzas/parmas in." You're educating them on the release *and* positioning your food as part of the plan.
