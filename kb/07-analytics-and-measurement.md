## Why measure

The frustrations this solves: *"I think my agency is doing a good job, but I honestly don't know what's working." "I'm spending on ads but can't tell if they're effective." "Am I improving or just wasting effort?"*

Principles:
- **Time is money.** If your posts are getting one like (and it's your mum), reallocate the hours. Heresy from a marketer: **one purposeful post a week that performs beats daily posting to crickets.**
- This program's strategies are deliberately **organic-first** — hospitality owners spend enough already; maximise free reach before paying.
- Decisions follow from three questions: What's our **customer behaviour** doing (more bookings, foot traffic, promoted items selling, customers posting about us)? Which campaign had the best **return** (marketing effectiveness)? Where should resources go next (**growth opportunities** — e.g. TikTok is fun, but if your audience isn't the young/US-heavy TikTok demographic, hours spent there are wasted)?

## The three core analytics categories

### 1. Awareness metrics — and the agency red flag

| Metric | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Reach** | The number of **unique accounts** (people/IPs) your content was shown to. 40 people on a Zoom call = reach of 40. |
| **Impressions** | Times people **stopped and paused on the content for 3+ seconds**. |
| **Views** | The **total number of times** the content appeared on screens — including ten appearances to the same scrolling person who never looked. |

So: 1,000,000 views with a reach of 10,000 means 10,000 people accumulated those million appearances.

**Red flag:** when a paid campaign underperforms, agencies lean on reach and impressions — "look how many people we reached for your $1,000!" Reach alone means nothing. Insist on conversion reporting (below).

### 2. Engagement metrics

- **Likes** — each one tells the algorithm to show that person your next post.
- **Shares** — the most valuable: a fan's friends are usually in your exact demographic, so shares are free targeted distribution. Case: an "open letter to the town" post about a venue's expansion got 22 shares natively + 8 more in a local group = 30 shares, a huge reach spike, and a **direct correlated revenue increase** that month.
- **Comments** — conversation = interest = algorithmic favour. (Respond to all of them — file 06.)
- **Click-through rate (CTR)** — how many people clicked your link (post links, story links, Link tree). The number that bridges engagement to money.
- **Dwell time** — fancy term for how long someone spends consuming your website/content. Longer = they're finding value = Google ranks you higher. Set up website analytics if you haven't; those numbers look after you.
- **Terminology note:** Meta reports "**content interactions**" — interactions sit *under* the engagement umbrella and feed the **engagement-rate** calculation (engagement relative to audience/reach). Track your rate monthly in a self-audit.

### 3. Conversion metrics — the ones that pay rent

- **Online orders & reservations.** Are they rising? Context matters: if foot traffic is down across the board (as many venues report) but online orders **hold steady**, your online presence is strong and doing its job.
- **Website visits, month on month.** Rising visits = your brand's online presence is building (socials create the curiosity; the website catches it).
- **Customer sign-ups** — app downloads, loyalty-program joins, VIP-list opt-ins. People raising their hands to hear from you.
- **Function & catering enquiries** — if you sell them, count them.

Set the standard with anyone who runs your marketing: **objectives and KPIs tie to revenue** — orders, reservations, enquiries — not "we converted 10,000 views into 500 interactions."

## Event & ad ROI: count the ad spend as an expense

The trap (real example): an owner ran paid ads to fill **free** events. Decent turnout, "a few hundred dollars" of revenue. Walk the maths: revenue − COGS − event wages − overheads − **the ad spend itself** = roughly **$15**. Nobody had counted the ads as an expense. Would you run that event again for $15? Before repeating any paid push, run: *(event revenue) − (COGS + wages + overheads + ad spend)* and judge the remainder honestly.

**Where paid money works (when you do spend):** high-intent search beats interruption — Google Ads for catering/functions outperform Facebook ads because searchers book on the spot (file 01); LinkedIn ads can target exact job titles for corporate catering (file 04).

## Keeping marketers honest (agency or in-house)

- **Ask for a written strategy.** If your social media manager has never written one, asking forces them to think like a strategist — and shows you whether their goals match yours.
- **Demand revenue-tied KPIs:** function enquiries, catering enquiries, online orders, award wins that drive sales — not follower counts, post counts, or views.
- **The $100 test:** "I'll pay you $100 to write a quarterly marketing strategy for the business." Cheap diagnostic; they might blow you away.
- Healthy scepticism: "it's their job to make things look good" — reach-heavy reports without conversion numbers are the tell.

## Q&A — Analytics session

**Q: When I scroll venues' feeds, food posts get 1–2 likes and the one team photo gets 30–100. Why?**
A: **People love people — we're biologically wired for it.** Lean in: attach a team member to every special and drink feature. Bonus: when a staffer stars in a post that pops, the dopamine of being liked buys you team culture — they come to work pumped. (This is also exactly what post-tracking in file 06 reveals.)

**Q: Recap the 9-post strategy?**
A: Picture the pyramid upside-down: **4 posts** engaging/entertaining/educating, **3 posts** community & connection (introduce team, staff, customers), **2 posts** "this is how you buy from us / book with us." (Full framework: file 06.)

**Q: I'm in the CBD and want corporate catering. Should the cafe have a LinkedIn? Should I run it?**
A: Yes to LinkedIn — it's where the booking decision-makers (office admins, EAs, events coordinators) live. **No, don't run it yourself** — you're a busy multi-venue operator. Post in the team chat: "who has LinkedIn experience?", task them with clear expectations and a target list ("connect with the office admins at [the big builders/firms nearby] and offer site catering and meeting coffee"). Delegated with clear goals, it gets done 10× better. LinkedIn's paid ads can also target specific job titles. (Full LinkedIn playbook: file 04.)

**Q: A member's account was hacked — what now?**
A: Raised on the call; the team committed to sending account-recovery resources. (Operational support item, not framework content.)

**Closing benchmark from the session:** members should be able to talk engagement rate the way they talk COGS — "my COGS are 28%, my engagement rate is 5%." Fluency in both sides is the goal of the six weeks.
