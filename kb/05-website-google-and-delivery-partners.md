## Part 1 — Your website

The foundation of online visibility. It must instantly communicate who you are, what you offer, how to order, and where the menu is. It doesn't need to be flashy — it needs to **work**, be legible, and be simple. Audit yours against every item below (and assign who fixes each item — you, or an email to your web developer).

### URL & domain
- Your own domain (yourbusiness.com / .com.au) — no free-tier "…/wixsite" or "…/square.site" suffixes. Free subdomains erode trust and conversion. Keep it short, clear, your business name.

### Favicon
- The tiny tab logo (from your brand asset suite, file 02). With 20–50 tabs open, an unreadable favicon = an invisible brand. Google "how to change favicon on [Squarespace/Wix/WordPress]" for the steps.

### Menu bar / navigation
- The **hamburger menu** must contrast with the background on *mobile* (many sites lose it entirely on phones).
- Place it **top-right**; chat widgets bottom-right — that's the right-handed thumb zone. Same logic for the Order Online button: one easy thumb-tap, no hunting.
- **Click every link on every page** (home, menu, contact). Roughly half of audited client sites had broken links leading nowhere.

### Mobile optimisation (non-negotiable)
~73% of Australian consumers (≈80%+ in the US) search for restaurants on mobile — but owners build and admire sites on desktop. Switch to mobile view: photos blur, text hides behind images, transparent-background buttons vanish over photos. Fix all of it.

**The Grade-One test:** build as if for a six-year-old. Big standout buttons, capitalised labels (MENU, ORDER ONLINE), nothing clever. Literally hand your phone to a young kid: "find the menu for me." If they can't, a non-tech-savvy adult can't either.

### What the site must contain
- **An enticing image** of your genuine strength — the fit-out, the signature dish the whole town knows, the long-serving team the community loves.
- **An online menu — as a page, not a PDF.** A PDF pulls users off your site (or downloads into the void), worsening your bounce rate; a menu *page* keeps them on-site longer, which tells Google your site is valuable and **ranks you higher**. (Lower bounce rate = good.) PDFs are also painful to read on phones.
- **Prices: strategic choice.** Worried about price-shopping against competitors? Remove prices from the online menu — people then choose you for the offer, not the cheapest eat, and price rises stop requiring website edits. Proud of your prices? Keep them.
- **Trading hours listed day by day** — write out Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday… individually *even if identical*, because skimming customers search the page for the one word ("Wednesday") that answers their question. "Mon–Thu 8–4" makes them work.
- **Clickable location.** Embed the maps pin (every builder has the block): one tap opens Google/Apple Maps with distance, drive time and public transport. Keep them on your site for everything else.
- **Functions, catering & contact:** **never** hide these behind a bare enquiry form. Asking people to hand over their details with zero information first loses them — bounce-rate comparisons prove packaged-info pages out-convert "submit an enquiry" pages. Publish the packages. Reframe scary terms & conditions as "the nitty gritties — all the extras you might be wondering about".
- **Homepage banner = most profitable action.** Whatever makes you the most money (dine-in bookings, direct online ordering) owns the top banner with a clear button.

### Examples worth studying (public sites cited in session)
- **Betty's Burgers** — textbook hamburger menu (Menu, Locations, Rewards, Gift Cards, Contact, big Order Online button); they reportedly invest heavily in a menu people *photograph and post* — branding as social proof.
- **Coffee Dose Cafe** — everything on one mobile page, no clicking needed.
- **Sandstone Point Hotel (Comiskey Group)** — multi-offer venue with crystal-clear layout.
- **The Komo (Redcliffe)** — a functions page genuinely optimised to convert; study it if you sell functions.
- **The Glenmore (Sydney)** — virtual venue tour (huge for function conversions) and a "NYE 2026 sign-up" a year out, which manufactures exclusivity ("they must be *that* busy"). One-tap call button.
- **Pink Moon Saloon (Adelaide)** — one scrollable page containing every contact path: clickable address, email, phone, gift cards, menus, socials, enquire-now.
Don't reinvent — **replicate** what's clearly working, one page-improvement task per week.

## Part 2 — Google Business Profile

Think of it as **a free ad in a massive catalogue**. Incomplete or outdated = customers unsure you're even still operating. Audit:

### Profile basics
- **Profile photo:** the *same* logo/favicon as Facebook, Instagram and your website favicon. Cohesion = trust.
- **Bio:** a one-sentence version of your Brand Positioning Statement. Shortcut: paste the BPS into ChatGPT — "turn this into one quick sentence for my Google listing." Gold-standard example: Flying Fish (Pyrmont, Sydney): "Modern seafood, sustainable menu, in an industrial-chic spot with water views" — one line and you can already picture it. (Counter-example in session: a waterfront cocktail bar with no bio at all — Google users had no idea what it was.)
- **Trading hours + special hours:** enter holiday hours **early** (Easter, ANZAC Day, Australia Day) — especially tourist destinations, where people plan trips weeks ahead. Special-hours field lives in the hours section. Monthly reminder: 1st of each month.
- **Links:** website, menu **page** link, social profiles, and your **current reservation system**. Common costly bug: a dead OpenTable link still attached while the venue moved to NowBookIt — bookings silently fail and guests show up unbooked. Check it today.
- **Service options:** tick everything that applies (outdoor seating, private dining room, vegan dishes, great cocktails, reservations required…). These become **search keywords** — "Pyrmont private dining" surfaces the venue that ticked the box.
- **Category & blurb:** if Google's auto-generated descriptor is stale ("coffee, burgers, cakes" when you now do dinner), use **Suggest an edit** — ask friends, family or your team to submit corrections/additions (dinner service, wheelchair access) from their own accounts.

### Photos
- Monthly photo dumps: wide shots of the venue (people want to preview the space) + staple menu items that never leave the menu.
- **Avoid uploading LTO photos as the business** — someone arriving for a long-gone special is a bad first visit.
- Can't delete customers' ugly photos, but you can **report** them (three-dot menu); Google removes them if it agrees.

### Reviews — respond to all of them, good and bad
Activity signals you're open, operating, and listening. Strategy notes:
- **People report what they dislike before what they like.** The classic: a three-year regular leaves their first-ever review — one star — over a single bad visit. Respond by *pointing out the history*: "Thank you for your support over the years — as you know from previous visits, this is uncommon for us. We're sorry we dropped the ball this once; we'd love to see you back." Readers immediately notice the imbalance ("three years of good visits and *this* is the review?"). Your response can flip how a one-star review is read — word it deliberately.
- (Follow your business coach's review-handling training for policy; the above is the marketing lens.)
- **Promote your great reviews.** Google surfaces the most-reacted-to reviews first. Drop a link to a glowing review in the team chat (or the family group chat): "give this a like / mark it useful." More staff hours come from more customers — it's a team effort. The best reviews then sit on top for every future reader.

## Part 3 — Delivery partners (Uber Eats, DoorDash, Menulog, etc.)

### The mindset shift
Delivery platforms are **not there to make you money; they're there to make themselves money — their job for you is telling the world you exist.** Treat them as a glossy catalogue / additional marketplace where people discover you, then engineer the path to ordering direct.

### The economics (run these numbers)
- **Price everything ~30% higher on the platforms.** It's a *convenience tax* — the customer is paying for not picking it up, and it offsets the ~30% commission (≈33% with GST).
- **Stop funding promotions.** "Free delivery": if delivery was $8.99 and the order totals $100, *you* pay the delivery **and** the ~33% commission on the full $100. "Buy one get one free": two $20 burgers sold for $10 — commission is charged on the **$20**, not the $10 you received. Rule of thumb: if monthly promo spend exceeds **~10% of your delivery revenue**, then after ~30% COGS plus wages and rent you're clearing roughly **$0–$1 per order**.
- **The direct-delivery alternative:** services like **Deliverect** dispatch Uber/DoorDash/Menulog drivers for orders placed on *your own website*. You keep 100% of the order; delivery costs ~$10 flat, most of which you pass on as a $6.99-ish fee — so a $50–60 order costs you ~$3 to deliver instead of ~$18 in commission.
- **Promote direct ordering everywhere:** website banner, Facebook cover, captions — "Order direct through us and save up to 30%." People remember it next time they're hungry.

### Optimising your delivery-app profile (audit checklist)
- [ ] **High-quality photo on every single item.** All major platforms offer a **free professional photo shoot when you sign on — take it**, and ask for the file folder afterwards (they hand it over) so you can reuse the images everywhere.
- [ ] **Hero image** matches your website's hero image; **logo** matches every other profile. One favicon, one logo, everywhere.
- [ ] **Descriptions** on every item, written for keywords (call out house-made relishes, gluten-free batter — "if you house-make it, say so").
- [ ] **Dietary tags in the item TITLE**, not just the tag field: "Lamb Kebab (DF)", "(GF option)". Skimmers — especially the one person ordering for a group of five — read titles only. If an item has a GF alternative, make it the **first** modifier option when clicked.
- [ ] **Translate authentic names in the title:** keep the authentic item name and add a plain-English bracket — e.g. a Dutch "patatje oorlog (loaded fries)". The more on-the-nose, the higher the conversion (the presenter once ordered beef tartare expecting a beef-flavoured tartare *sauce* — don't make customers guess).
- [ ] **Trading hours accurate, including public holidays.** A customer who waits 45 minutes on a public holiday before the order auto-declines (because you never updated holiday hours) is gone forever. In Deliverect: Menu → Optimisation → Holiday Hours. Five minutes of due diligence.
- [ ] **No flyers in or on delivery-partner bags — ever.** Platforms permanently cancel accounts for it. Flyers belong on your *direct* orders only.

**Proof it works:** a dark-kitchen client whose Uber Eats profile was fully optimised this way (pro photos, keyword descriptions, dietary tags in titles) does hundreds of thousands of dollars a month through delivery platforms alone.

## Q&A — Get Found Online session

**Q: Should we respond to reviews on the delivery platforms too?**
A: Only when warranted — to thank someone and build connection, or when you were genuinely at fault (e.g. plastic in food: respond, refund or credit through the platform). Note: delivery-platform reviews generally **aren't publicly visible** to other customers the way Google reviews are, so the reputational stakes differ.

**Q: Google's auto-summary of my business is outdated (says cakes/no dinner). How do I fix the blurb?**
A: Update your categories and attributes in the Google Business dashboard, and have friends/family/team use **"Suggest an edit"** on the public listing to remove stale items and add new ones (dinner service, accessibility). Multiple suggestions accelerate the change.

**Q: My bio/service options don't display the way other venues' do.**
A: Display varies by how complete the profile is and how Google chooses to render it; short, sharp bios are likelier to surface high. Fill every field (bio, services, attributes, links) and give it time. (The presenter committed to publishing a full walkthrough video — building a dummy listing field-by-field — for members.)

**Q: How long should the Google bio be? Mine feels too long.**
A: One sentence. Paste your BPS into ChatGPT: "Turn this into one quick sentence for my Google listing."

**Q: Our menu items have authentic foreign names. Title or description for the translation?**
A: Both — authentic name first, plain-English descriptor in brackets **in the title**, detail in the description. Easier understanding = higher conversion on that item.
